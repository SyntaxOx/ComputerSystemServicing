    - Machines have the caability of dealing with variety of problems, and are able to act in response to programs created to meet different needs.

    - Designed to perform one specific task classification by type of data handled

    - Deals with quantities that are continously variable, they give only approximate results.

    Computer Generations

    First Generation Computers (1951-1959)

    • Used vacuum tubes for the circuit.
    • Magnetic Drums for memory.
    • Very expensive to operate.
    • Uses a great amount of electricity.
    • Produced a large amount of heat.
    • Relied on the machine language.
    • Can solvethe one problem at a time.
    • Can't do the multitasking work.
    • Input was based on the punched cards and paper tape.
    • Output was displayed on the printouts.
    • ENIAC and UNIVAC are examples of the First Genaration of the computer.

    Second Generation Computers (1959-1965)

    • Transistors replaced the vacuum tubes.
    • Very reliable than the first generation.
    • Input was based on the punched cards and paper tapes.
    • Output were displayedon the printouts.
    • Symbolic or Assembly language.
    • Second generation computer was developed for the Atomic Energy Industry.

    Third Generation Computers (1965-1971)

    • Integrated circuits were used.
    • Transistors were placed on the silicon chips known as Semiconductor.
    • User used the keyboard and monitors.
    • Interfaced with OS (Operating System).
    • Run many applications at one time.
    • Computer is small, cheaper and reliable the it's predecessors.

    Fourth Generation Computers (1971-2010)

    • The Microprocessor brought in the fourth generation of computer.
    • Thousands of the intergrated circuits were built on the single silicon chip.
    • Computer fit in the palm of the hand.
